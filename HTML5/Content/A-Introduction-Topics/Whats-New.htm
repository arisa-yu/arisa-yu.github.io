Understanding Accessibility.

The World Health Organization defines disability as a complex interaction between a person’s body and the society they live in. Overall, disability is not just a medical condition, but often the result of the environment failing to accommodate human diversity. Two major models help explain this distinction.

The Medical Model

The medical model views disability as an abnormality that is a problem within the individual. A person’s body or mind is measured against a “normal” standard, like having 20/20 vision, and if it falls outside the standard, then it is considered impaired. The solution, based on this model, is to diagnose, treat, and rehabilitate or adapt said person. In other words, the goal is to “fix” the person by providing them with corrective vision lenses, medical treatments, or other specialized programs.

While medical support is important and can be essential in many cases, the medical model can unintentionally reinforce stigma by labeling people with disabilities as less capable.

The Social Model

The social model shifts the responsibility away from the individual and towards society, and argues that the problem lies within the environment. People are no disabled by their body or mind, but rather by inaccessible systems and attitudes of those around them.

For example, a wheelchair user is not disabled by their wheelchair, but by entrances with no ramps; a deaf person is not excluded because of their inability to hear, but by the lack of sign language knowledge within their community.

This model emphasizes that the solution should not be “fixing” the individual with the impairment, but to redesign the world in an inclusive way. This means that accessibility is not an accommodation, but rather a design responsibility, especially for writers and content creators.

Accessible Design

Accessible design focuses on making products usable for people with disabilities by adding features as adaptations. For example, offering a button that makes the text on a website bigger, added captions after a video is uploaded online, or building a ramp on the side of a building. While this helps prevent exclusion, this can unintentionally push the idea that accessibility is an afterthought.

Universal Design

Universal design, which Ronald Mace created, aims to make environments accessible for the widest range of people without implementing adaptations. For example, entrances of buildings would be at ground-level and the typography on websites would already be readable and high-contrast. This concept keeps disability in mind from the beginning, and rather than asking how we can fix something for users with disabilities, we place more of an emphasis on how we can properly design things so fewer people are excluded.
